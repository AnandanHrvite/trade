Trend Pullback (TREND_PB)

Wait for a clear trend, let the price dip back to a good buying spot, then buy an option only when the trend proves it is starting again. Buy into strength — never guess a turn. Explained here in everyday language.

1. The idea in one minute

Picture a river already flowing one way. Now and then the water eddies backwards for a moment, but the river keeps going. This strategy waits for a market that is clearly "flowing" in one direction, waits for a small backward eddy (a pullback), and buys the moment the flow starts pushing forward again.

  • If NIFTY is in a clear uptrend and dips then turns back up → we buy a CE (call option) to profit from the rise.
  • If NIFTY is in a clear downtrend and bounces then rolls back down → we buy a PE (put option) to profit from the fall.

We buy one option at a time — no pairs, no hedges — and we are picky: on most days nothing looks good enough and we simply don't trade. The whole point is to join a move that is already working, not to be clever and guess a top or a bottom.

The three questions, in order. Every trade must answer "yes" to all three, one after the other:
① Is there a real trend? (15-min chart) → ② Did it pull back to a good price? (5-min chart) → ③ Is the trend starting again? (resumption candle) → buy a slightly-ITM option
If any question is "no", we wait. Most of the day is spent waiting — that is the strategy working as designed.

A whole trend-pullback day, at a glance. Price climbs in the morning, then dips back toward the orange EMA20 (yellow dot) — a healthy pullback that stays inside the purple VWAP band. A green resumption candle triggers BUY CE, and the position rides the afternoon trend until the trail closes it near the top (EXIT, about +110 spot points). This is one clean example day — most days show no such setup and we don't trade.

One important quirk: no volume check

Many strategies confirm a move by checking trading volume (how many contracts changed hands). The NIFTY index itself has no real tradable volume — it's just a number, not a thing you buy. So instead of faking a volume figure, this strategy measures conviction a different way: how big the candle is. A decisive, full-bodied candle is our proof that buyers (or sellers) really showed up. This is deliberate — see Step 3.

2. Words you'll see (plain meanings)

A handful of terms come up again and again. Here's each one in everyday language — keep this nearby and the rest reads easily.

TermWhat it really means
CandleA price bar for a slice of time, showing where NIFTY opened, closed, and its high and low in that slice. A "5-minute candle" covers 5 minutes; a "15-minute candle" covers 15. A green candle closed higher than it opened; a red one closed lower. The candle's "body" is the fat part between open and close.
Trend biasThe market's overall direction right now — up, down, or unclear. We work it out from the slower 15-minute chart, and we only ever trade in the same direction as the bias.
Higher-high / higher-lowHow we spot an uptrend by eye: each peak is taller than the last peak, and each dip bottoms out higher than the last dip — like climbing a staircase. A downtrend is the mirror image (lower highs and lower lows), a staircase going down.
EMA20 / EMA50Two smoothed average-price lines. EMA20 follows the last ~20 candles, EMA50 the last ~50, so EMA20 reacts faster. When the fast line (EMA20) sits above the slow line (EMA50) and is rising, the trend is healthy and up. We use them as a trend "health check", not as the trigger.
VWAPThe day's fair-value line — roughly the average price so far. Above it, buyers are in charge; below it, sellers. We only take CE trades above the line and PE trades below it.
PullbackA short move against the trend — the backward eddy in the river. In an uptrend it's a small dip; in a downtrend a small bounce. It's the discount we're waiting to buy at.
Resumption candleThe one candle that tells us the pullback is over and the trend is pushing on again. It's our buy trigger (Step 3).
ATR"Average True Range" — simply how much NIFTY normally moves in a candle. If ATR is 20, a typical 5-minute candle spans about 20 points. We use it as a ruler: a "big" move on a calm day is small on a wild day, and ATR tells us which is which.
Chandelier trailA trailing stop-loss that hangs a fixed distance below the highest price the trade has reached (like a chandelier hanging below the ceiling). As price climbs, the stop climbs with it, but it never drops back — it only ratchets one way. It's how we let a winner run yet still lock in gains.
BreakevenMoving the stop-loss up to our entry price once a trade is nicely in profit. From that point the worst case is a zero — the trade can no longer become a loss.
R (R-multiple)"R" is the amount we risked on the trade — the distance from our entry to our first stop-loss. "1R of profit" means we've made as much as we first put at risk. It's a simple way to size profit against risk without rupees.
Time-stopA rule that exits a trade that has gone nowhere for too long. Options quietly lose value as time passes, so a stuck trade is a slow bleed — better to free up and move on.
ITM / OTM"In-the-money" (ITM) options already have real value baked in, so they move almost point-for-point with NIFTY and decay slowly. "Out-of-the-money" (OTM) options are cheaper lottery tickets that decay fast. We buy slightly ITM because it tracks a trend far better.
PremiumThe price you pay for the option (in ₹ per unit). "Premium fell 35%" means the option lost about a third of its value.
VIXThe market's "fear gauge." High VIX = jumpy, expensive options and bigger swings. We can tell the strategy to sit out when VIX is too high.
OI (open interest)How many option contracts are currently held open — a read on where the big money is positioned. An optional filter can use it to avoid buying into a wall of opposing bets. Off by default.

3. Step 1 — Read the big-picture trend

Before anything else, the strategy zooms out to the 15-minute chart (each candle covers 15 minutes) and asks one question: is there a genuine trend right now? If the answer is no, the day is over before it started — we don't trade.

For an uptrend, all four of these must be true at once. A downtrend is the exact mirror.

CheckIn plain words
1. Staircase upThe recent peaks and dips form a rising staircase — a higher high and a higher low. This is the main test; it's the price telling us the direction, not an indicator.
2. Fast line above slow lineThe EMA20 line is above the EMA50 line — the faster average is leading the way up.
3. Fast line risingThat EMA20 line is actually tilting upward, not flat or drooping.
4. Above fair valuePrice is above the day's VWAP (fair-value) line — buyers are in control today.
Why so many checks? Any one of them can be fooled on its own — a single tall candle, a fluke crossing. Demanding the price staircase and two moving-average health checks and fair-value all agree is what keeps us out of choppy, going-nowhere days. If the checks disagree, the trend is "unclear" and we simply wait. To avoid being tricked by a half-finished bar, the strategy only reads 15-minute candles that have fully closed.

4. Step 2 — Wait for the pullback

Once the big picture says "clear uptrend", we do not chase the price higher. Chasing means buying at a bad, stretched price. Instead we drop to the faster 5-minute chart and wait patiently for the price to dip back toward the EMA20 line — the "good buying spot". Think of it as waiting for a sale instead of paying full price.

But not just any dip. The pullback has to be a healthy pause, not a collapse:

  • It must actually reach the discount zone. The dip has to come back down and touch the EMA20(5m) area. A move that stays stretched far above it never gave us a good price, so we pass.
  • It must be a real pause, not a one-candle flick. At least a couple of candles need to lean against the trend — a genuine breather, not a single wick.
  • It must not fall too far. If the dip drops more than 1.5× the normal candle move (ATR) below the EMA20 line, that's not a pause — the trend is probably breaking. Too deep = we walk away.

What we DON'T take. The trend was up, but the dip crashed straight through the EMA20 line and kept going — more than 1.5× a normal candle (about 30 pts on this calm day) below the line. That's not a pause, it's a break. The yellow dot marks the "too deep" candle: no trade. Skipping setups like this is most of the job.

Why measure the dip against ATR instead of fixed points? A 25-point dip is nothing on a wild day but a big warning on a calm one. By comparing the dip to how much NIFTY is actually moving today (ATR), the same rule keeps making sense in any market — you never have to re-tune numbers when volatility changes. The strategy also measures the dip from the peak of the little run-up (not from the start of the window), so "how deep" reflects the true give-back.

5. Step 3 — The resumption candle (the trigger)

A pullback alone isn't a reason to buy — the dip could keep going. We wait for one clear candle that says "the pause is over, the trend is back on." That's the resumption candle, and it's the moment we actually buy. For an uptrend (CE), the just-closed 5-minute candle must tick every box below.

The resumption candle must…In plain words
Be green and push upIt closes higher than it opened — buyers won that candle.
Have a solid bodyIts body is at least 0.5× the normal move (ATR). A big, decisive body is our stand-in for a volume check — it's the proof that buyers really showed up, not just drifted.
Reclaim the trend lineIt closes back above the EMA20(5m) line — price has climbed out of the dip.
Break the previous candle's highIt closes above the high of the candle just before it — a fresh push, not a sideways shuffle.

We buy on that candle's close — a confirmed, finished candle — never on a mid-candle spike (a "wick") that might vanish. For a downtrend it's the mirror: a red candle with a solid body that closes below EMA20(5m) and below the prior candle's low, and we buy a PE.

This is the "no volume" design in action. Because the NIFTY index has no genuine volume figure to lean on, the size of this candle's body is the conviction test. A big body means real buying pressure; a puny body means the bounce is half-hearted and we skip it. Simple, honest, and it can't be faked.

6. Step 4 — Pick the option

Once all three questions are "yes", we still have to choose which option to buy and make sure it's worth trading.

CheckDefaultIn plain words
Which option1 step in-the-moneyA CE in an uptrend, a PE in a downtrend, shifted one strike inside the money (roughly a "delta 0.6" option). This option already has real value, so it follows NIFTY almost point-for-point and loses value slowly — far better for riding a trend than a cheap far-out option.
Price range₹120 – ₹400Skip if the option is too cheap (a near-worthless lottery ticket) or too pricey. Keeps us in options that actually move well.
Buy/sell gap≤ 2 pointsSkip if the gap between the option's buy price and sell price is wide — a wide gap quietly eats your profit just to get in and out.

Position size is a fixed number of lots — we do not bet bigger on "better-looking" setups (that kind of confidence-scaling is deliberately avoided until it's proven out-of-sample). While the trade is open, the option's price is re-checked every few seconds.

7. Step 5 — When we sell

The exits are built to let a winner run while cutting losers quickly. There is no fixed profit target — we never say "+40% and I'm out." And there are no partial exits — we don't sell half and keep half. The whole position rides until one of the rules below fires, and whichever fires first closes the trade.

A key detail: almost every exit watches the NIFTY index price (spot), not the option's own price. Spot is steadier and cleaner to trail. The one exception is the last-resort premium backstop.

OrderWhat sells us outDefaultIn plain words
1Starting stop-lossclamped 8–30 ptsRight after we buy, our safety line sits at the bottom of the pullback dip (the top of the bounce, for a PE). If price falls back through it, we're out. The distance is kept sensible — never tighter than 8 points, never wider than 30.
2Move to breakeven1R profitOnce the trade is up by as much as we first risked (1R), we slide the stop up to our entry price. From here the trade can't turn into a loss.
3Chandelier trail2.5× ATRThe main winner-runner. The stop hangs 2.5× the normal move below the best price reached, and climbs as price climbs — but never drops back. We ride the trend and only give back a little at the end.
4Trend brokeEMA20(5m)If a 5-minute candle closes back through the EMA20 trend line, the move is over — we exit.
5Going nowhere (time-stop)6 candlesIf we've held for about 6 candles (~30 minutes) and the trade is still flat, we cut it loose rather than bleed value to time-decay.
6End of day15:15Anything still open at 3:15 PM is sold, win or lose. We never hold overnight.
7Option melted down−35%The one option-price rule: if the premium collapses 35% from what we paid (say a sudden gap), we bail immediately, whatever the spot is doing. A disaster backstop.

A worked example — a CE trend day

Real numbers, one clean trade. Suppose today a typical 5-minute candle spans about 20 points (ATR5 ≈ 20). Here's the whole trade as one chart — the dip to the EMA20, the green resumption candle where we buy, and the trailing stop that finally takes us out near the top.

The full trade. Price ran up, dipped back to the orange EMA20 line (yellow dot, ~24,248), then a solid green candle reclaimed the line and broke the prior high → BUY CE at 24,272. The chandelier trail (2.5×ATR) rode the climb to a 24,384 peak and only sold when price slipped back to it → EXIT 24,334, +62 spot points. Follow it row-by-row below.

TimeNIFTY spotWhat happens
10:15~24,270Big picture (15m): peaks rose 24,180 → 24,260 (higher high), dips rose 24,120 → 24,190 (higher low). EMA20 (24,230) is above EMA50 (24,205) and rising. Price is above VWAP (24,240). → Clear uptrend — look for CE only.

10:4524,272Resumption candle: a green candle opens 24,254, closes 24,272. Body = 18 pts, above the 10-pt minimum (0.5×20). It closes above EMA20(5m) 24,252 and above the prior candle's high 24,265. → BUY a CE at spot 24,272. The option (1-step ITM) costs ₹190.
10:45stop 24,248Starting stop = the dip low, 24,248. Risk = 24,272 − 24,248 = 24 points (that's our 1R). Well within the 8–30 clamp.
11:1024,296Trade is +24 pts — up by a full 1R. → Stop lifts to breakeven (24,272). The trade can no longer lose.
11:4024,360Price keeps climbing. The chandelier trail now sits at best − 2.5×20 = 24,360 − 50 = 24,310, ratcheting up with each new high.

The tally. Spot rose from 24,272 to 24,334 = +62 points. A slightly-ITM option moves about 60% of that, so the premium climbed roughly 62 × 0.6 ≈ +37 points, from ₹190 to about ₹227. With one NIFTY lot of 75 units, that's about (227 − 190) × 75 ≈ ₹2,775 profit — from a trade we entered after the trend proved itself, with the downside capped at breakeven the whole back half of the ride.
Why no fixed target and no partials? If we always sold at "+40%", or sold half early, we'd shrink the few big trend days — and those big days are exactly what pay for all the small losing ones. The chandelier trail keeps us in the move as long as it lasts, while the breakeven and the −35% backstop make sure a bad trade dies small. The skill is in the entry; the exit just decides how much of a good entry we get to keep.

8. The "market too jumpy" filter (VIX)

VIX is the market's fear gauge. When it's high, options get expensive and price swings turn violent — a riskier time to trade. Trend Pullback has its own optional VIX filter to sit out those days. It's off by default, so VIX is ignored unless you switch it on.

SettingDefaultIn plain words
TREND_PB_VIX_ENABLEDfalseThe on/off switch for the VIX filter. Off by default.
TREND_PB_VIX_MAX_ENTRY22If VIX is above 22, don't take new trades today — too jumpy. (Falls back to the shared global limit if not set.)

9. Safety limits & the losing-streak brake

Several limits protect the account — think of them as circuit-breakers of growing size: per day, and across a bad run.

LimitDefaultIn plain words
Trades per day3At most 3 entries in a session. The strategy is picky by design — most days it takes fewer, often none.
Daily loss brake₹5,000Once the day's losses reach ₹5,000, stop taking new trades for the day.
Losing-streak brake3 sessionsAfter 3 losing sessions in a row, pause new entries to cool off and reset. (Set to 0 to turn off.)

It adds up today's realised profit/loss across all six strategies; once the combined loss hits the cap, new entries are blocked in every strategy for the rest of the day — not just this one. 10. Paper, Live & Backtest

Paper — /trend-pb-paper (practice)

Real live prices, pretend money. No real orders are ever placed. This is the official version of the strategy — whenever live or backtest behaves differently, paper is treated as correct. Every trade is saved to your trading-data folder so you can review it later.

Live — /trend-pb-live (real money)

Real orders through Fyers — but it works by wrapping the paper engine. When paper decides to buy or sell, live places the matching real order. That means live and paper can never drift apart: they are the same brain. It is triple-gated so it can't fire by accident.

A real Fyers order fires only when all three are true: TREND_PB_LIVE_ENABLED=true and the global LIVE_HARNESS_DRY_RUN=false and TREND_PB_LIVE_DRY_RUN is not true — plus a valid Fyers login. Out of the box it's in dry-run: it prints the order it would have placed and sends nothing. Recommended path: watch it on paper, confirm live and paper match on a recorded /replay session, and only then consider flipping the gates.
Survives a restart (crash-recovery). When a trade opens, its details are written to a small file (.active_trend_pb_position.json) and wiped when the trade closes. If the server restarts while a trade is still open, on boot the system reads that file back, checks it against the real broker position, and either picks the trade back up or — if the broker shows nothing open — clears the stale note and sends a Telegram alert so a forgotten "orphan" position can't sit unmanaged. (This is the same crash-recovery every other strategy now has.)

Backtest — /trend-pb-backtest (test on the past)

Runs the strategy over past dates to see how it would have done. Since history has no option prices, it estimates the option (starting from a typical slightly-ITM price of ₹240 and modelling how it moves), and it subtracts a realistic buy/sell-cost haircut each way so the results don't flatter themselves. It also does two honesty checks:

  • A "dumb baseline" to beat. It re-runs the same dates with a naive version (just enter in the trend direction at the window open, no pullback filter). If the real strategy can't beat the dumb one, its clever filters were just noise.
  • Walk-forward testing. Trades are split into rolling out-of-sample chunks, and thin chunks (too few trades) are flagged — because a "win" in a tiny sample is luck, not proven edge.
  • Honest stop-fills on gaps. If a candle gaps straight past the stop-loss, the backtest fills at the worse gap-open price, not the stop price — so a jumpy day isn't flattered by pretending you got out exactly at the stop.

12. What's still missing

  • Backtest option prices are estimated. History has no option quotes, so backtest profit is a close approximation, not exact fills. Always sanity-check against paper and /replay.
  • It trades rarely — on purpose. The strategy waits for a real trend, a real pullback, and a real resumption all lining up. Many days it takes nothing. Judge it over weeks, not a single afternoon.
How it fits with everything else. Trend Pullback is independent of every other strategy — it shares only the single Fyers price feed and the common safety/cost/option-picking plumbing. ORB and all the other strategies are untouched by it.
