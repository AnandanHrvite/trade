⚙ 1. The idea in one minute

Picture two things drawn on NIFTY's 5-minute chart. First, a fast wiggly average line (the EMA 9) that hugs the price closely. Second, a calm "fair value" channel down the middle of the day (the VWAP band) — think of it as the price range the market has been treating as normal so far today.

Most of the time the fast line stays inside that channel. This strategy waits for the moment it bursts out of the channel — because that usually means the market has stopped drifting and started to commit to a direction.

  • If the fast line pushes out of the top of the channel → buyers are taking over → we buy a CE (call option) to profit from a rise.
  • If the fast line pushes out of the bottom of the channel → sellers are taking over → we buy a PE (put option) to profit from a fall.

We hold one option at a time (just a CE or just a PE — never a pair). And here's the unusual part: there is no fixed profit target and no fixed stop-loss. We simply hold the trade until the fast line drops back inside the channel — that's the market telling us the burst is over. The whole point is to catch a real move and stay in it as long as it lasts.

Where the prices come from. Live NIFTY prices arrive from Fyers (the same single price feed every strategy here shares). When you run it for real money, the actual buy/sell orders go through Zerodha. Everything is on the 5-minute chart.

📚 2. Words you'll see (plain meanings)

A few terms come up again and again. Here's what each one means, in everyday language — keep this handy and the rest of the guide reads easily.

TermWhat it really means
CandleA 5-minute price bar showing where NIFTY opened, closed, and its high/low in those 5 minutes. A "green" candle closed higher than it opened; a "red" one closed lower. We only make decisions when a candle finishes ("on candle close").
CE / PECE (a call) makes money when NIFTY goes up. PE (a put) makes money when NIFTY goes down. We only ever hold one of them at a time.
EMA 9A fast smoothed average of the last 9 candle-closing prices. It's the wiggly line that hugs the price. Because it only looks back 9 candles, it reacts quickly — that's why we use it to spot a move starting. (The "9" is just how many candles it averages; it's adjustable.)
VWAPThe day's fair-value line — roughly the average price so far today. If price is above it, buyers have had the upper hand today; below it, sellers have.
Standard-deviation band / sigma (σ)"Sigma" is just a measure of how spread-out the day's prices have been — a normal-wobble size. We draw two lines, one sigma above the fair-value line and one sigma below, forming a channel. Price sitting inside the band = normal. Price breaking out of it = an unusually strong push. "±1σ" means one normal-wobble in each direction.
CrossWhen one line moves from one side of another line to the other side. Here it's the fast line (EMA 9) moving from inside the channel to outside it (our buy signal), or back from outside to inside (our sell signal).
Session-anchoredThe fair-value line and its band are built up fresh each morning from the market open and thrown away at the end of the day. "Anchored" at 9:15 means the maths starts counting from the opening bell every day.
PremiumThe price you pay for the option, in ₹ per unit. "Premium fell 20%" means the option lost a fifth of its value.
ITM / OTMIn-the-money (ITM) options already have real value baked in and move almost point-for-point with NIFTY. Out-of-the-money (OTM) options are cheaper "lottery tickets" further from the current price. This strategy just buys the standard at-the-money option (the strike nearest the current price).
EOD square-off"End Of Day square-off" — selling whatever is still open before the market closes, win or lose. We never hold overnight.
VIXThe market's "fear gauge." High VIX = jumpy, expensive options and bigger swings. The strategy can sit out when VIX is too high.
OI (open interest)How many option contracts are currently open — a read on where the big money is positioned. Used as an optional live-only safety check.
Opposite-side cooldownA short "cooling-off" timer after we exit a trade, during which we're not allowed to immediately flip to the opposite side (CE→PE or PE→CE). It stops the strategy from flip-flopping and losing on both sides during a choppy patch.
HLC3Just a candle's (High + Low + Close) ÷ 3 — a "typical price" for that candle. It's the number the fair-value line is built from.

📈 3. The two lines we watch

The whole strategy runs off just two things drawn on the 5-minute chart. Everything else is a reaction to how these two interact.

The fast line (EMA 9)

This is a smoothed average of the last 9 candle-closing prices. Because it only looks back a short way, it turns quickly and stays close to the actual price. Think of it as a "where is price leaning right now" line. It's the line that does the crossing.

How the "cross" is judged. The strategy doesn't just ask "is the fast line above the channel right now?" — it compares two candles in a row: was the line inside the channel last candle, and is it outside this candle? That "was in, now out" change is the actual signal. This avoids reacting to a line that was already outside and just wandering along.

The fair-value channel (VWAP ± one sigma)

Each morning, starting at 9:15, the strategy begins averaging every candle's typical price (HLC3) to build the day's fair-value line (VWAP). It also tracks how spread-out those prices have been — that spread is "one sigma." It then draws:

Fair-value line   = the day's running average price
Top of channel    = fair-value line + (1 × the normal wobble)
Bottom of channel = fair-value line − (1 × the normal wobble)

The channel resets every morning — yesterday's numbers don't carry over. And it gets a bit narrower or wider through the day as the market calms down or speeds up, so it always reflects today's conditions.

SettingDefaultWhat it does
EMA9VWAP_EMA_PERIOD9How many candles the fast line averages. Smaller = faster and twitchier; larger = smoother and slower.
EMA9VWAP_BAND_MULT1How wide the channel is, in "normal wobbles." 1 = one wobble each side (the standard setting). Set it to 0 and the channel collapses to a single fair-value line — then we'd just trade the fast line crossing the plain VWAP.
EMA9VWAP_VWAP_SESSION_START09:15The time each morning the fair-value maths starts counting from (the market open).
One honest caveat about the fair-value line. A "proper" VWAP weights each price by how much traded there. But the NIFTY index itself doesn't publish real trading volume on the feed, so this line treats every candle equally instead. In practice it looks and behaves almost identically to the version you'd see on TradingView — the shape and the cross timing are exact — but the precise number may be a point or two different. It's the crossing that matters, and that is spot-on.
Why the channel is warm before we start trading. The maths starts at 9:15, but we don't take our first trade until 10:30 (see Section 4). That head-start means the fair-value line and its channel are already settled and reliable by the time we act on anything.

▶ 4. When we buy (the cross)

There's no long checklist here like some other strategies — a single event triggers the trade: the fast line breaking out of the channel. We wait for the 5-minute candle to finish before acting (so a brief poke mid-candle that pulls back in doesn't fool us).

CE
Fast line breaks out the TOP → buy a CE
Last candle the fast line was at or below the top of the channel; this candle it closes above the top. Momentum is breaking out to the upside — we buy a call.
EMA9 was ≤ top line, now closes above it
PE
Fast line breaks out the BOTTOM → buy a PE
The mirror image: last candle the fast line was at or above the bottom of the channel; this candle it closes below it. Momentum is breaking down — we buy a put.
EMA9 was ≥ bottom line, now closes below it

The break-out entry. The fast line (green EMA 9) sat inside the blue VWAP ±1σ channel while price drifted, then on one candle it closed above the top band → BUY CE at that candle's close (24,318). We held with no stop and no target while the move ran. It ended on a sharp reversal candle — a red candle that closed below the lows of both prior candles → EXIT (24,334), even though the fast line was still outside the band.

Strong vs weak break-outs

Not every break-out is equal. When the fast line pops out of the channel, the strategy now measures how far it pushed past the edge — measured in "normal wobbles" (sigma), so the yardstick shrinks or grows with the day's volatility. A break of at least 0.25 of a wobble past the edge is graded STRONG; a smaller poke is graded WEAK (likely just noise nudging over the line).

A real-numbers example. Say today's normal wobble (sigma) is 28 points, so the strong-break threshold is 0.25 × 28 ≈ 7 points past the edge. If the fast line closes 11 points above the top of the channel (about 0.4 of a wobble) → STRONG. If it closes just 3 points above (about 0.1 of a wobble) → WEAK.
Filtering out the weak ones is optional and OFF by default. With EMA9VWAP_STRENGTH_FILTER turned on, WEAK crosses are skipped entirely (no trade) — you get fewer, higher-conviction entries. Left OFF (the default), every break-out still trades exactly as before, but the log now records whether it was STRONG or WEAK so you can see the quality of each entry. The grading happens inside the shared strategy brain, so paper, live and backtest all agree. The threshold itself is adjustable via EMA9VWAP_STRONG_MIN_SIGMA (default 0.25).

STRONG vs WEAK. Same channel, two break-outs. On a calm patch the fast line barely nudged over the top band — the plain yellow dot marks that WEAK poke (about 3 pts past the edge, well under the 0.25σ ≈ 7-pt bar). Later the line closed a decisive 11 pts above the band → a STRONG break. With the optional strength filter OFF (the default) both would trade; turn it ON and the WEAK poke is skipped.

We only trade in the middle of the day

The channel starts building at 9:15, but the strategy waits until 10:30 to take its first trade and stops taking new trades after 14:30 (2:30 PM). The first hour is often noisy and directionless, and the last hour is too close to the bell to give a move room to run.

SettingDefaultWhat it does
EMA9VWAP_ENTRY_START10:30Earliest we'll open a new trade — skips the noisy first hour.
EMA9VWAP_ENTRY_END14:30Latest we'll open a new trade. A trade that's already open can keep running past this time — only fresh entries stop here.

Exactly when the buy fires

By default we buy on the close of the candle that did the crossing — no waiting for a second candle to confirm, and no jumping in mid-candle. Two optional settings can change that, but both are off out of the box:

SettingDefaultWhat it does when turned ON
EMA9VWAP_INTRACANDLE_ENTRYfalseBuy the instant the line crosses, without waiting for the candle to finish. (Off = wait for the close, the safer default.)
EMA9VWAP_CONFIRM_CANDLE_ENABLEDfalseDemand one extra candle pushing the same way before buying — more proof, but a later entry. (Off = act on the cross candle itself.)
Reading the trade in the log. Each entry writes a line like EMA9 24312.5 crossed ABOVE VWAP top 24298.1 (VWAP 24270.4 +1σ). That tells you the fast-line value, the channel edge it crossed, and the fair-value line and band that defined it — a fingerprint that identifies this strategy's trades in a mixed log.

📍 5. When we sell

This is the unusual heart of the strategy. When we buy, we set no stop-loss, no profit target, and no trailing stop. We're deliberately giving the move room to breathe. The trade stays fully open until one of the exits below fires. On every finished candle the strategy checks these in order, and the first one that matches sells the whole position.

1
A sharp reversal candle  on by default
If the candle that just closed slams hard against us, we bail early — we don't wait for the slower signal exit. For a CE: a red candle that closes below the lows of both of the previous two candles. For a PE: a green candle that closes above the highs of both previous candles. That kind of candle is a clear "the move just flipped" warning.
EMA9VWAP_REVERSAL_EXIT_ENABLED = true
2
The main exit — the fast line folds back inside the channel
This is the core "the move is over" signal. A CE is sold when the fast line drops back below the top of the channel. A PE is sold when the fast line climbs back above the bottom. Once the line is back inside the normal band, the reason we entered is gone — so we're out.
CE: EMA9 back below top line  |  PE: EMA9 back above bottom line
3
Sell before the close
Anything still open at 15:15 (3:15 PM) is sold, win or lose. This happens a little before the market close so we're never caught holding into the bell.
time reaches EMA9VWAP_EOD_EXIT_TIME (15:15)
4
Final backstop — the engine shuts down
At 15:30 the strategy stops for the day. If somehow anything were still open, it's closed here. This is the last-resort net behind the 15:15 sell.
time reaches EMA9VWAP_STOP_TIME (15:30)
So the "let it run" exit isn't the only fast way out. Because the sharp-reversal exit (rule 1) sits ahead of the main signal exit, a violent candle against us can close the trade before the fast line has folded back inside the channel. If you'd rather hold purely to the channel signal, turn EMA9VWAP_REVERSAL_EXIT_ENABLED=false.
Why no fixed target or stop? A fixed "+30% and I'm out" target would cut the big trend moves short — and those big moves are exactly where this style makes its money. And a tight stop-loss would keep getting nicked by normal wobble. So instead of a fixed number, the market itself tells us when to leave: the fast line folding back inside the channel means the burst has run out of steam.

🚫 6. Extra emergency stops (off by default)

Out of the box, none of the following are active — the strategy ships as a pure "let the move run" engine. These are optional safety catches you can switch on if you want extra protection or want to experiment. Each one is an add-on that sits on top of the four exits in Section 5.

Extra stopSettingDefaultWhat it does when you turn it on
Option-price stop EMA9VWAP_OPT_STOP_PCT 0 Sell if the option's price falls this fraction below what you paid (e.g. down 30%). Catches a fast bleed even if the chart signal hasn't flipped yet. 0 = off.
NIFTY-points stop EMA9VWAP_STOP_LOSS_PTS 0 Sell once NIFTY itself has moved this many points against you. A hard line in the sand. 0 = off.
Candle-trailing stop EMA9VWAP_CANDLE_TRAIL_ENABLED / _BARS false / 3 Trail a stop behind the recent candles — for a CE it follows the lowest low of the last few candles upward, tightening only, never loosening. _BARS = how many candles it looks back.
Not-working-yet cut EMA9VWAP_NEG_CANDLE_LIMIT 0 Give up on a trade that's still in the red after this many candles (winners are left alone to keep running). 0 = off. (A stray code comment says "default 2" — ignore it; the real default is 0.)
Old time-based stop EMA9VWAP_SL_MODE ema Set this to candle to bring back an older "you've held it long enough, get out" timer. Left at ema (the default), that timer stays switched off and the signal exit runs the show.
Why leave them off? The edge of this strategy is patiently holding a clean move to its natural end. A tight stop would chop the winners short and defeat the whole purpose. These levers are here as insurance for live trading or for testing ideas — not as part of the core plan.

🛡 7. The "market too jumpy" filter (VIX)

VIX is the market's fear gauge. When it's high, options get expensive and prices swing violently — a riskier time to trade. This strategy can sit out those days.

An important quirk: the VIX limits here are the SHARED, global ones. Most other strategies have their own private VIX dials. EMA9+VWAP does not — it uses the global VIX_* settings (the same ones the EMA_RSI_ST strategy uses). So if you change VIX_MAX_ENTRY to loosen or tighten it here, you're also changing it for that other strategy. There is no EMA9VWAP_VIX_* dial to adjust this strategy on its own.
SettingDefaultWhat it does
VIX_FILTER_ENABLEDtrueThe on/off switch. On by default — so the VIX check is active unless you turn it off.
VIX_MAX_ENTRY20If VIX is above 20, don't take any new trade — too jumpy, options too pricey.
VIX_STRONG_ONLY16Between 16 and 20, only take the very best ("strong") signals. Now that break-outs are graded (Section 4), a WEAK break in this VIX band is skipped — only a STRONG break trades.

Two more live-only safety checks

When trading real money, two extra guards can run before a buy goes through:

  • OI (open interest) check — a read on where the big money is positioned.
  • Bid-ask spread check — skips an option whose buy price and sell price are too far apart, because that gap quietly eats your profit on the way in and out.

Both only apply on the live path, and only when you switch them on. Practice mode and backtests don't use them.

Two counters checked before every trade. Before acting on any signal, the strategy also checks the day's trade count and the day's loss total (Section 8). If either has hit its limit, it simply stops trading for the rest of the day.

🛑 8. Safety limits & cooldowns

Several limits protect the account and stop the strategy from over-trading during a rough patch. Think of them as brakes of increasing strength.

BrakeSettingDefaultWhat it does
Most trades per dayEMA9VWAP_MAX_DAILY_TRADES20After this many trades in a day, stop for the day.
Daily loss brakeEMA9VWAP_MAX_DAILY_LOSS₹5,000Once this strategy's losses reach ₹5,000, take no more trades today.
All-strategies loss capPORTFOLIO_MAX_DAILY_LOSS0 (off)A shared brake across all six strategies at once. Once the combined loss of the whole bot for the day reaches this number, no strategy — including this one — may open a new trade for the rest of the day. 0 = off.
Losing-streak breaker—automatic3 losses in a row → a 5-minute pause (about 4 candles). A longer 15-minute version shuts the strategy for the day. A single winning trade clears the pause.
Chop guardEMA9VWAP_MAX_CONSEC_LOSSES0Optionally sit out the rest of the day after this many straight losses. 0 = off. Any winner resets the count.
Same-side cool-offEMA9VWAP_SL_PAUSE_CANDLES3After a stop-loss is hit, block re-entering the same side for a few candles. This only matters if you've switched on one of the optional stops in Section 6 — the normal signal exit isn't a stop-loss, so it doesn't trigger this.
Opposite-side cool-offEMA9VWAP_OPPOSITE_SIDE_COOLDOWN_ENABLED / _CANDLEStrue / 3After an exit, block flipping to the opposite side (CE→PE or PE→CE) for 3 candles — stops the strategy whipsawing back and forth in choppy conditions.
The all-strategies loss cap is OFF by default. PORTFOLIO_MAX_DAILY_LOSS starts at 0, which means "no shared cap." When you set a number, it adds up today's actual (booked) profit and loss across every strategy the bot runs and, once the combined loss reaches that number, quietly blocks any new entry everywhere for the rest of the day. It's a pure brake — it never places or changes an order, and if it can't read the numbers for any reason it lets trading continue rather than freezing the bot.
Why the opposite-side cool-off really matters here. Remember the sharp-reversal exit (Section 5, rule 1): a CE can be closed by a hard red candle — but that very same candle is exactly what would trigger a fresh PE buy. Without the cool-off, the strategy could sell its CE and instantly buy a PE straight into the same messy candle, and lose on both. The 3-candle timer makes it wait for the chop to pass before re-engaging.

🧮 9. A worked example with real numbers

Here's a textbook CE trade on a trending morning, with all the default settings (channel width 1σ, trade window 10:30–14:30, sharp-reversal exit on). Follow the fast line and the top of the channel:

10:20  The channel right now:
         Fair-value line  = 24,270
         Normal wobble    = 28 points
         Top of channel   = 24,270 + 28 = 24,298
         Bottom of channel = 24,270 − 28 = 24,242
       Fast line (EMA9) = 24,291  →  still INSIDE the channel. No trade yet.

10:35  This candle CLOSES. The fast line has climbed to 24,313,
       and the top of the channel is now 24,301.
         Last candle: fast line 24,296 was BELOW the top (24,299)
         This candle: fast line 24,313 is ABOVE the top (24,301)
       →  That's the break-out. BUY 1 CE at this candle's close.
          No stop-loss, no target set — we just hold.
       Log: "EMA9 24313 crossed ABOVE VWAP top 24301 (VWAP 24274 +1σ) [STRONG]"

11:00–12:40  NIFTY keeps grinding up. Every 5-min candle closes with the
       fast line still poking above the top of the channel. We do nothing —
       just let the winner run. No trail to tighten, no target to hit.

Now the trade ends the moment ANY ONE of these happens first:
  (a) A red candle closes below the lows of BOTH previous candles
      →  sharp-reversal exit; we sell at that candle's close.
  (b) The fast line finally drops back BELOW the top of the channel
      →  main signal exit ("the burst is over"); we sell.
  (c) Neither happens all afternoon  →  we sell at 15:15 no matter what.

Say the option was bought at ₹120 and we exited via (b) at ₹185.
That's +₹65 per unit of premium — the kind of clean run this style is built to catch.

After we exit, the PE (opposite) side is locked for 3 candles, so a single
angry red candle can't instantly flip us into a losing put.
The whole edge in one line: get in the moment the fast line punches a full normal-wobble out of the fair-value channel, then step back and let the move run — ended only by a hard reversal candle, the line folding back inside the channel, or the closing bell.

⚙ 10. Paper, Live & Backtest

Paper — /ema9vwap-paper (practice)

Real live prices, pretend money. No real orders are ever placed. This is the "official" version of the strategy — whenever live or backtest behaves differently, paper is treated as correct and the others are fixed to match it. Every trade is saved to your trading-data folder, with a day-by-day audit file, and (when tick-recording is on, which it is by default) every price tick is stored so you can replay the exact day later.

It now survives a crash mid-trade. When a trade is open, the strategy writes a small snapshot of it to disk (.active_ema9vwap_position.json), and clears it the moment the trade closes. If the bot restarts while a trade is still open — a deploy, a crash, a machine reboot — it reads that snapshot on start-up and warns you (with a Telegram message) that a position was open before, so it never gets silently forgotten. Earlier this snapshot wasn't actually being written, so a crash could leave an untracked position; that gap is now closed for all six strategies.

Live — /ema9vwap-live (real money, "Live = Paper")

The live page runs the exact same brain as practice mode — it just also places real Zerodha orders when it decides to buy or sell. It's locked behind two safety switches so it can never fire by accident: EMA9VWAP_LIVE_ENABLED must be on and the global dry-run switch (LIVE_HARNESS_DRY_RUN) must be off. There's also a per-strategy switch, EMA9VWAP_LIVE_DRY_RUN, that keeps just this one strategy in "pretend" mode even while others are trading live — it logs the order it would have placed but sends nothing. Only turn the real dry-run off after you've confirmed on a replayed day that live and paper make the same decisions.

Backtest — /ema9vwap-backtest (test on the past)

Runs the strategy over past dates to see how it would have done. It was built to copy the practice-mode decisions exactly — the sharp-reversal exit, the signal exit, the end-of-day sell, the cool-offs, the VIX and daily-loss brakes were all carried across — so the backtest reproduces the real behaviour rather than roughly approximating it.

The backtest now charges a realistic trading cost. It used to fill every trade at the perfect price (no slippage) — the only backtest here that did, which made its past results look rosier than the others. It now assumes you lose about 1.5 points on each side of a trade to the bid-ask gap (BACKTEST_SLIPPAGE_PTS, default 1.5), matching every other strategy's backtest. Expect slightly lower backtest numbers than before — they're just more honest now.

Replay — /replay (re-run a recorded day)

Takes a day you recorded in practice mode and runs it again, tick by tick. You can replay it with the original settings (to reproduce exactly what happened) or with your current settings (to test a tweak against the same real prices, after market hours). It's the safe way to try changes before trusting them.

⚠ 12. What's still missing

  • It's a new strategy with no track record yet. Added 28 June 2026, with no history of tested tweaks behind it. Gather a batch of clean practice sessions before changing any setting — there's no proven baseline to compare against yet.
  • The sharp-reversal exit can be a little trigger-happy. On choppy days it may sell out quickly, and combined with the opposite-side cool-off, it can leave you sitting out a good re-entry. Watch for over-eager exits before deciding whether to keep EMA9VWAP_REVERSAL_EXIT_ENABLED on.
  • You can't tune VIX just for this strategy. It shares the global VIX limits with another strategy, so changing them here changes them there too. There's no private EMA9VWAP_VIX_* dial.
  • The fair-value line is an approximation. Because the NIFTY index has no real volume on the feed, this "VWAP" is really an equal-weighted average. The channel and the crossing are exact, but the printed VWAP number can be a point or two off what you'd see on TradingView — don't expect them to match to the point.
