01 The idea in one minute

Picture a rubber band drawn around NIFTY's recent price — a stretchy upper edge and a stretchy lower edge that move with the market. Most of the time the price bounces around inside that band. Once in a while it pushes hard enough to close outside the band. That sudden push is what this strategy hunts for.

  • If a 5-minute candle closes above the top edge of the band, and everything else agrees, we buy a CE (Call option) — a bet that NIFTY keeps rising.
  • If a candle closes below the bottom edge, we buy a PE (Put option) — a bet that NIFTY keeps falling.

But a price poking out of the band isn't enough on its own — lots of those pokes snap straight back and lose money. So before buying we insist on two more things agreeing: a trend tool called SuperTrend must be pointing the same way, and a momentum meter called RSI must be strong enough. Three green lights, or no trade. Then we make it wait for one more candle as proof. All of these terms are explained in Section 2 — keep reading, nothing here is assumed.

The version running today: "V7" (SuperTrend edition)

This is the current, up-to-date design. The one big change to know about: the strategy's trend tool used to be something called Parabolic SAR (PSAR). PSAR is gone. A single tool, SuperTrend, now does all the trend work by itself — it decides the direction, it sets the starting stop-loss line, and it fires the "the trend has turned, get out" exit. If you have read an older copy of this guide that mentions PSAR, ignore those parts: everything below describes V7.

02 Words you'll see (plain meanings)

A handful of terms come up again and again. Here's what each one means in everyday language. Keep this section handy and the rest of the guide reads easily.

TermWhat it really means
CE / PECE (a Call) makes money when NIFTY goes up. PE (a Put) makes money when NIFTY goes down. We only ever buy one of them at a time — never both.
PremiumThe price you pay for the option, in ₹ per unit. If you buy a Call at a premium of ₹120 and later sell it at ₹175, you made ₹55 per unit. Options trade in fixed "lots" (NIFTY is about 75 units per lot), so ₹55 × 75 ≈ ₹4,125 for one lot.
CandleA 5-minute price bar. It shows where NIFTY opened, where it closed, and the highest and lowest it reached in those 5 minutes. A "green" candle closed higher than it opened; a "red" one closed lower. We make decisions when a candle closes.
Bollinger Bands (BB)Two stretchy lines drawn above and below a moving average of price — like an elastic channel that widens when the market is jumpy and narrows when it's calm. Price closing outside a band is a sign of an unusually strong push (a "breakout"). We use a tight setting so the bands hug the price closely.
BreakoutWhen price shoves past a barrier — here, closing above the top band or below the bottom band. It's the spark that starts a trade.
SuperTrendA single line that flips from one side of the price to the other to tell you the trend. When the line sits below the price, the trend is up (bullish); when it sits above the price, the trend is down (bearish). When it "flips" to the other side, the trend has turned. It's the strategy's one trend tool — it replaced the old PSAR.
RSIA momentum meter that runs from 0 to 100. High readings (near 70+) mean strong upward push; low readings (near 40 or below) mean strong downward push; the middle is indecision. We use it to make sure a breakout has real force behind it.
Initial stop-loss (SL)A pre-set price line where, in principle, we'd give up on the trade. Here it's set to the SuperTrend line at the moment we buy. In V7 it's mainly a reference for measuring risk — the real exits are the ones in Section 7.
Hard stopA last-resort safety exit measured in points. If the trade goes badly wrong — NIFTY moves 30 points against us — we bail no matter what. It's set deliberately wide so it only ever catches a genuine disaster, not normal wiggles.
Profit lockA way of protecting a gain. Once a trade is nicely in profit, we watch the peak, and if it gives back half of that peak we take the money and leave. As the peak grows, the protected floor rises with it (it "ratchets"). Explained with numbers in Section 7.
Confirmation candleOne extra candle we insist on before buying. The candle that lights up all three signals only "arms" the trade; we buy on the next candle only if it keeps pushing the same way. This weeds out fake pokes.
Bid-ask spreadThe gap between the price to buy an option and the price to sell it. A wide gap quietly eats your profit just from entering and exiting, so we skip options with a wide spread.
VIXThe market's "fear gauge." A high VIX means jumpy, expensive, unpredictable conditions. We can tell the strategy to sit out when VIX is too high.
ATM (at-the-money)The option whose strike price is closest to where NIFTY is trading right now. This strategy buys the ATM Call or Put.

03 The three things we watch

Every 5 minutes, when a candle closes, the strategy re-reads three tools. All three have to agree before it will even think about buying. Here they are, one at a time.

1. Bollinger Bands — the stretchy channel

Setting: 20-candle average, band width 1 (tighter than usual)

The middle line is the average price over the last 20 candles. The upper and lower bands sit one "standard deviation" away — a fancy phrase that just means "a typical amount of wiggle." Most charts use a width of 2; we use a tighter 1, so the bands hug the price and a breakout registers sooner. When a candle closes above the upper band, that's the raw spark for a Call. Closing below the lower band is the spark for a Put.

2. RSI — is there real force behind the push?

Calls need RSI above 70  |  Puts need RSI below 40

RSI is a momentum meter from 0 to 100. A breakout with weak momentum tends to fizzle, so we add a single cut-off. For a Call, RSI must be above 70 (strong upward push). For a Put, RSI must be below 40 (strong downward push). That's it — one number for each side, not a range. (An optional extra, off by default, can also require RSI to be actively moving the trade's way.)

3. SuperTrend — the trend tool that does three jobs

Setting: SuperTrend (10, 3) — the sole trend source in V7

SuperTrend is a single line that sits on one side of the price and flips to the other side when the trend turns. When the line is below the price, the trend is up; when it's above the price, the trend is down. In this strategy it does three jobs, and it's the only trend tool involved:

  • Job 1 — confirms direction. A Call needs the line below the close (up-trend); a Put needs it above the close (down-trend). If the band breaks but SuperTrend disagrees, no trade.
  • Job 2 — sets the starting stop-loss line. When we buy, the SuperTrend line's value becomes our initial stop-loss reference (used to measure risk, not as a twitchy tick-by-tick stop).
  • Job 3 — fires the trend-flip exit. If, on a later candle close, the line flips to the wrong side of price, the trend has turned against us and we sell.

This is the big V7 change. Older versions used PSAR for these jobs. PSAR has been removed from the code entirely; SuperTrend is always on and there is no toggle to switch trend tools any more.

Before any of this runs

The strategy needs some history to compute these tools, so it waits for at least 30 candles after the open before it will take any signal, and it only opens new trades between 9:21 AM and 2:30 PM IST. Outside that window it just watches.

04 The entry checklist

For the strategy to arm a trade on a candle close, all of the boxes below have to say "yes." Miss one and it waits for the next candle. Here they are for both sides, side by side.

Arming a CE (bullish)

1
The candle closes at or above the upper Bollinger Band.
2
SuperTrend is bullish — its line sits below the close.
3
RSI is above 70 — strong upward push.
4
The SuperTrend line is not too far away — no more than 50 points from the close (see below).
5
Starting stop-loss line = the SuperTrend value; we'll buy an ATM Call.

Arming a PE (bearish)

1
The candle closes at or below the lower Bollinger Band.
2
SuperTrend is bearish — its line sits above the close.
3
RSI is below 40 — strong downward push.
4
The SuperTrend line is not too far away — no more than 50 points from the close.
5
Starting stop-loss line = the SuperTrend value; we'll buy an ATM Put.

Why the "not too far away" check matters (step 4)

Right after SuperTrend flips, its line can sit a very long way from the price — sometimes 100+ points. Since that line is our starting stop-loss, a far-away line means a huge amount of room to lose. So the strategy simply skips any entry where the line is more than 50 points from the close (setting BB_RSI_MAX_ENTRY_SL_PTS). It caps the risk without needing a made-up tight stop. Set it to 0 to turn the check off.

Optional filters (off unless you switch them on): a "chop gate" (BB_RSI_ADX_ENABLED) can block all trades on sideways, directionless days by requiring a trend-strength meter called ADX to be above 20 — the strategy wins in trends and bleeds in chop, so this makes it sit out ranging sessions. And BB_RSI_RSI_TURNING can require RSI to be actively moving the trade's way, not just sitting past the threshold.

When there is nothing to trade. On a calm, low-VIX day the bands (tight setting of 1) collapse right onto the price — the whole channel is barely 12 points wide. Price keeps nudging its edges, and the yellow dot marks a candle that pokes a whisker above the top band. But that "break" is just noise inside a 20-point range, not a real push — so no trade. This is the regime the strategy sits out; the ADX chop gate above exists precisely to skip days like this.

05 Wait for proof, then buy (the confirmation candle)

Here's the important part: the candle that ticks all the boxes above does not buy anything. It only arms the trade — like cocking a spring. We then look at the very next candle, and we buy only if it keeps pushing the same way.

What "the next candle confirms" means (both checks on by default)

With BB_RSI_CONFIRM_CANDLE_ENABLED and BB_RSI_CONFIRM_OUTSIDE_BAND both on (the defaults), the next candle must do two things before we buy:

  • Close beyond the arming candle's close — i.e. push even further in our direction, not stall or reverse.
  • Still be outside the band — genuinely broken out, not slipped back inside.

If both hold, we buy at that second candle's closing price. If the next candle stalls or slips back inside the band, the spring is released and nothing happens — a fake poke costs us nothing. (Turning BB_RSI_CONFIRM_OUTSIDE_BAND off makes it enter the moment price crosses the arming level during the next bar; turning BB_RSI_CONFIRM_CANDLE_ENABLED off buys straight on the arming candle with no wait. The defaults are the careful setting.)

A band-break trade, start to finish. Price closes above the upper Bollinger Band (yellow dot = the arming candle). The next candle pushes further and stays outside → BUY CE at 24,060. The move runs for a few candles, then a candle closes back inside the band — the breakout has failed, so the band-re-entry exit fires → EXIT at 24,044. (Here SuperTrend never flipped and the 30-point hard stop was never hit; the band re-entry got us out first.)

06 Pick the option

Once we've decided to trade, we still have to choose which option to buy and check it's worth buying.

CheckSettingIn plain words
Which strikeATMWe buy the at-the-money Call (for CE) or Put (for PE) — the strike closest to where NIFTY is right now.
Buy/sell gap≤ 2 pointsSkip the option if the gap between its buy and sell price is wider than 2 points (MAX_BID_ASK_SPREAD_PTS) — a wide gap quietly eats profit. If quotes are missing, this check lets the trade through rather than blocking it.
One at a time—Only one BB_RSI option is ever open at once. No pairs, no stacking.

In the backtest there are no real option prices. History doesn't record option quotes, so the backtest estimates how the option would have moved with NIFTY (using a delta of about 0.55 and a daily time-decay of ~10 points). Backtest profit is therefore a good approximation, not the exact fills you'd get live — always sanity-check against paper and replay.

The backtest now models the rough edges too. If price gaps straight past the hard stop — a candle opens well beyond it — the exit is filled at that worse gap price, not pretended at the stop line, so a jumpy bar's real loss isn't understated. And every exit now pays a small slippage haircut (BB_RSI_SLIPPAGE_PTS, default 1.5 points) on top of the entry-side one, because options fill a touch worse than the spot-implied price. Both changes make backtest results a little more honest and a little less rosy.

07 When we sell

Once we're in a trade, several exit rules watch it at once, and whichever one triggers first closes the position. They split into two groups: rules checked on every price tick (fast), and rules checked only when a candle closes (slower). The fast group runs first, so most exits happen intra-candle; the candle-close group is the backstop.

1
Band re-entry / band touch — checked every tick

If price slides back inside the band the breakout has failed, so we get out immediately — right at the band line, without waiting for the candle to finish. To avoid being knocked out by a stray wick on the entry candle itself, this only "arms" once the breakout has stretched at least 10 points past the band (BB_RSI_BB_REENTRY_ARM_PTS). Toggle: BB_RSI_BB_REENTRY_EXIT (on).

2
Wide hard stop — checked every tick

The disaster brake. If NIFTY moves 30 points against our entry (BB_RSI_STOP_LOSS_PTS), we're out no matter what. It's set wide on purpose so it never clips the normal small trades — it only catches the deep failures that would otherwise bleed to −100 points before a slower exit fires. Set to 0 to disable.

3
Profit lock — checked every tick

The upside exit, measured in NIFTY points. Once the trade's best move so far reaches 25 points (BB_RSI_PROFIT_LOCK_TRIGGER_PTS), a floor arms at 50% of that peak (BB_RSI_PROFIT_LOCK_PCT). If the gain slips back below the floor, we bank it and leave. The floor ratchets up as the peak grows (see the worked example). Because it's in points, it works even when live option prices are unavailable.

4
Band re-entry (candle close) + SuperTrend flip — checked at candle close

Two slower backstops. First, if a candle closes back inside the band, the breakout has failed and we exit. Then, if SuperTrend flips to the wrong side of price on the close, the trend has turned and we exit — this is what lets a strong winner ride for hours and then bows out only when the trend genuinely ends.

5
End of day

Anything still open is sold near 3:30 PM IST (TRADE_STOP_TIME), with an earlier safety square-off just before. We never hold a BB_RSI option overnight.

What we deliberately do NOT do: there is no fixed profit target, no trailing stop-loss that creeps up tick by tick, no "move the stop to break-even" step, and no time-based exit. The starting stop-loss line (the SuperTrend value at entry) is a reference for sizing risk — it doesn't trail. Profit is banked by the points-based profit lock; runners are handled by the candle-close SuperTrend flip.

A worked example — a CE trade, start to finish

10:05  A 5-min candle closes at 24,040. The upper band is at 24,035 — so it
       closed ABOVE the band. SuperTrend sits at 24,005 (below price, up-trend),
       35 pts away — within the 50-pt limit. RSI = 73 (above 70).
       All three lights green  →  the trade is ARMED. (We do NOT buy yet.)

10:10  The next candle closes at 24,058 — beyond the arming candle's close (24,040)
       AND still above the band.  →  Confirmed.  BUY an ATM Call.
       Premium paid: ₹120. Starting stop-loss line = SuperTrend 24,005 (for sizing).

10:35  NIFTY has climbed to 24,100 — a +60-point gain at its peak.
       Profit lock arms (peak ≥ 25) with a floor at 50% × 60 = +30 points.

11:20  NIFTY runs on to 24,160 — peak now +120 points.
       The floor RATCHETS up to 50% × 120 = +60 points.

11:45  NIFTY pulls back to 24,100. The gain has dropped to +60 points — it has
       touched the ratcheted floor  →  SELL, banking about +60 spot points.
       The Call, bought at ₹120, is now worth ~₹175, so ~₹55 per unit.
       At ~75 units per lot that is roughly +₹4,100 on one lot (before charges).

       The hard stop (30 pts against) and the SuperTrend flip never had to fire —
       the profit lock did its job.

And a losing example, kept small

Say instead the confirmed Call goes nowhere: NIFTY ticks up ~11 points (arming the band-re-entry exit at 10 points past the band) and then rolls over and slips back inside the band. The band-re-entry exit fires on the next tick, at the band line — so the trade is cut for a small loss instead of bleeding down to the 30-point hard stop. That fast structural exit is exactly why the hard stop can be set so wide: the common small failures are caught early, and the wide stop only exists for the rare runaway disaster.

08 The "market too jumpy" filter (VIX)

VIX is the market's fear gauge. When it's high, options get expensive, breakouts fail more often, and swings turn violent — a poor time for this strategy. The optional VIX filter lets it sit out those conditions.

VIX ≤ 16all signals allowed
16 – 20best setups only
VIX > 20no trades

VIX 16 or below — calm

Low fear, cleaner trends, tighter option prices. All valid breakout signals are allowed. This is the ideal backdrop for BB_RSI.

VIX 16 to 20 — elevated

Moderately nervous. Only the strongest setups are allowed through; borderline breakouts that would likely fail in the chop are skipped.

VIX above 20 — high

Fear zone. All entries blocked. Wide whipsaws and inflated premiums erode the edge; the strategy waits for VIX to cool.

How it's switched on. BB_RSI has its own VIX toggle, BB_RSI_VIX_ENABLED (off by default). Its two levels, BB_RSI_VIX_MAX_ENTRY (the "no trades above" ceiling) and BB_RSI_VIX_STRONG_ONLY (the "best only" line), fall back to the shared values 20 and 16 if you don't set them. If the VIX feed is unavailable, VIX_FAIL_MODE decides whether to block or allow (blocks by default).

New: a whole-book daily loss cap (off by default)

Every limit above only watches BB_RSI on its own. But you run several strategies at once, and on a bad day each one could hit its own cap while the account, all added together, loses far more. A new cross-strategy brake — PORTFOLIO_MAX_DAILY_LOSS (default 0 = off) — adds up today's realised profit-and-loss across all six paper strategies together. Once that combined loss reaches the cap, no strategy (BB_RSI included) may open a new trade for the rest of the day; trades already open are left alone. It only ever blocks new entries — it never places or changes an order — and if it can't read the day's numbers it lets trades through (fails open). Leave it at 0 to keep it off; set it (e.g. 12000) to arm it.

10 Paper, Live & Backtest

All three modes share the exact same signal and exit logic — the band break, the three lights, the confirmation candle, the profit lock, the hard stop, the band re-entry and the SuperTrend flip. They differ only in where the prices come from and whether real orders are placed.

Paper — the practice mode

bbRsiPaper.js • page /bb_rsi-paper

  • Real live prices from Fyers, but pretend money — no real orders.
  • This is the official version of the strategy: whenever live or backtest behaves differently, paper is treated as correct.
  • Every trade is saved to disk, so it survives a restart.

Live — real money

bbRsiLive.js • page /bb_rsi

  • Real orders through Fyers.
  • Locked behind two switches so it can't fire by accident: BB_RSI_ENABLED must be on and the global LIVE_HARNESS_DRY_RUN must be off.
  • While dry-run is on (the default), it logs the order it would place but sends nothing.

Backtest — test on the past

bbRsiBacktest.js • page /bb_rsi-backtest

  • Runs the strategy over past dates using cached historical candles.
  • Since history has no option prices, it estimates the option (delta ~0.55, time-decay ~10 pts/day) and includes charges.
  • Gives a downloadable report; treat the profit as an approximation.

Replay — re-run a recorded day

page /replay

  • Takes a day you recorded in paper mode and runs it again tick by tick.
  • Replay it with the original settings to reproduce exactly what happened, or with your current settings to test a tweak against the same real prices.
  • The safe way to try changes after market hours.

12 Notes & what's still missing

  • Paper is the source of truth. If live or backtest ever disagree with paper, paper is treated as correct and the other side is fixed to match — never the other way round.
  • Backtest option prices are estimated. History has no option quotes, so backtest profit is an approximation. Always confirm against paper and replay before trusting a result.
  • This strategy lives and dies by the trend. It makes money when the market picks a direction and runs, and it bleeds in choppy, sideways sessions. The optional ADX chop gate exists to sit those days out.
  • The hard stop is wide on purpose. 30 points sounds loose, but the fast band-re-entry exit catches the common small failures first — the wide stop is only there for the rare runaway. Don't tighten it without understanding that trade-off.

The big correction in this edition

Earlier copies of this guide described the trend tool as Parabolic SAR (PSAR). That is out of date. In the current V7 code, PSAR has been removed completely and SuperTrend (10, 3) is the sole trend source — it confirms the entry direction, sets the starting stop-loss line, and fires the trend-flip exit. Every default above was read straight from the live code.
