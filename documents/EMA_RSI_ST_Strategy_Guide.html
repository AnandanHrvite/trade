01 — The idea in one minute

This strategy is a trend-follower. It sits and watches NIFTY, and it only buys when four different signs all point the same way at once — a strong hint that the market has really picked a direction. Then it buys a single option and rides the move for as long as it lasts.

  • When everything points up → we buy a CE (a call option), which gains when NIFTY rises.
  • When everything points down → we buy a PE (a put option), which gains when NIFTY falls.

We only ever hold one option at a time — no pairs, no hedges. We watch 5-minute candles (a candle is one 5-minute price bar; more on that below). Once we're in a trade, we keep a safety line that climbs along behind the price. As long as the trend keeps going, we keep holding; the moment the trend clearly breaks, that safety line gets touched and we're out.

The everyday version

Think of merging onto a highway behind a fast car. You don't pull out until the road is genuinely clear — you want several green lights, not just one. Once you're following, you keep a safe distance behind the car and you tighten that distance as you go, so you can never fall too far back. You stay behind it until it clearly turns off — then you take your exit. The four entry checks are your "is the road clear?" and the moving safety line is your "safe following distance."

Live orders go through Zerodha; all the price data comes from Fyers. The maths never changes with the market's mood — the same rules run whether NIFTY is calm or wild.

02 — Words you'll see (plain meanings)

A handful of terms come up again and again. Here's each one in everyday language — keep this nearby and the rest reads easily.

TermWhat it really means
CandleOne 5-minute price bar. It shows where NIFTY opened, where it closed, and its high and low in those 5 minutes. A green candle closed higher than it opened; a red one closed lower. A "finished" (closed) candle is one whose 5 minutes are over.
CE / PECE (a call) makes money when NIFTY goes up. PE (a put) makes money when NIFTY goes down. We buy only one of them at a time.
Premium / LTPThe premium is the price you pay for the option, quoted per unit (e.g. ₹200). LTP just means "last traded price" — the option's price right now. If the premium falls from ₹200 to ₹170, the option lost ₹30 of value per unit.
LotOptions are bought in fixed bundles called lots. One NIFTY lot is 75 units, so a ₹10 move in the premium is ₹750 in your pocket (or out of it).
EMAA smoothed average of recent closing prices — a trend line that follows price with a slight lag. The number is how many candles it averages: EMA20 is smooth and slow, EMA9 is quick and jumpy, EMA50 is the slowest. When a faster EMA sits above a slower one, the trend is up.
RSIA 0–100 "momentum meter." Low (near 20–30) means the move down is tired; high (near 70–80) means the move up is tired. We want RSI strong enough to show real push, but not so extreme that the move is about to run out of steam.
SuperTrendA single line that flips from below the price (it's saying "trend is up") to above the price ("trend is down"). One clean yes/no read on which way the market is leaning.
Trailing stop (safety line)A price level that automatically closes the trade if it's hit. "Trailing" means it follows the price in the winning direction and only ever gets tighter — it never loosens. It's how we lock in more and more of a winner while capping a loser.
Break-evenMoving your safety line to the price you paid, so the trade can't turn into a loss. The old always-on version was removed; there is now an optional break-even stop you can switch on (off by default) — see Section 6.
PointsNIFTY index points. If NIFTY goes from 24,300 to 24,325, that's a 25-point move.
Bid-ask spreadThe gap between the price you can buy an option at and the price you can sell it at. A wide gap quietly eats your profit every time you enter and exit, so a live trade is skipped if the gap is too wide.
OI (open interest)How many option contracts are currently held open — a read on where the big money is positioned. An optional live-only check.
VIXThe market's "fear gauge." High VIX means jumpy prices and expensive, fast-moving options — a riskier time to trade. We can tell the strategy to sit out when VIX is too high.

03 — Where you run it: Paper, Live, Backtest

The exact same decision-making brain runs everywhere — only the plumbing around it changes. That's on purpose: it means practice, real trading, and testing can't quietly disagree with each other.

ModePageWhat happens
Paper/ema_rsi_st-paperPractice mode. Real live prices, but pretend money — no real orders are ever sent. This is treated as the official behaviour: if live or backtest ever disagree with paper, paper is the one that's right.
Live/ema_rsi_st-liveReal money through Zerodha. Locked behind two safety switches so it can't fire by accident (see Section 11).
Backtest/ema_rsi_st-backtestRuns the strategy over past days to see how it would have done. History doesn't store old option prices, so it estimates the option's price — a good approximation, not exact fills.
Replay/replayRe-runs a day you recorded, tick by tick, with either the original settings or your current ones — a safe way to test a change against real past prices after market hours.

The candle size is set by TRADE_RESOLUTION — usually 5 minutes, but 3 or 15 also work. Before it will trade at all in the morning, the strategy needs about 55 candles of history to "warm up" its trend lines, so it doesn't act on half-formed averages.

04 — Step 1: The 4-point entry checklist

Before any trade, all four checks below must be true — on the same finished 5-minute candle. We call that candle the signal candle. If even one check fails, we don't trade. (For a PE, every check is simply flipped upside-down.)

1
The trend lines are stacked the right way
The faster trend line (EMA20) has to sit above the slower one (EMA50) for a CE — that's the picture of an up-trend. There's an optional stricter version that also demands the very fast EMA9 be on top (EMA9 above EMA20 above EMA50), which throws out weak, flat setups.
CE: EMA20 above EMA50  |  (optional strict: EMA9 > EMA20 > EMA50)
2
Momentum is strong — but not exhausted
The RSI momentum meter has to be pushing our way, yet not so extreme that the move is about to tire out. For a CE it must be above the "real push" floor but below the "overbought, don't chase" ceiling.
CE: RSI above 52 and below 80  |  PE: RSI below 48 and above 20
3
SuperTrend agrees
The SuperTrend line must be on the "up" side for a CE (its line sitting below the price). This is the single clearest yes/no read on direction, and it's the strategy's main trend source.
CE: SuperTrend line below price (up)  |  PE: line above price (down)
4
The candle actually closes on the right side
The signal candle must close above the base trend line (EMA20) for a CE. This stops us from buying a dip that only looks bullish because older lines are still stacked — the price itself has to close on our side, right now.
CE: close above EMA20  |  PE: close below EMA20  (EMA9 if strict mode is on)
Buy a CE (call) when the market points UP
Bullish setup
1. EMA20 above EMA50 (trend up)
2. RSI above 52 and below 80 (strong, not overheated)
3. SuperTrend on the up side
4. Candle closes above the base EMA
→ first safety line = the previous candle's LOW
Buy a PE (put) when the market points DOWN
Bearish setup
1. EMA20 below EMA50 (trend down)
2. RSI below 48 and above 20 (strong, not overheated)
3. SuperTrend on the down side
4. Candle closes below the base EMA
→ first safety line = the previous candle's HIGH

A clean CE entry, start to finish. The blue EMA20 stays above the purple EMA50 the whole time — the up-trend is intact. Price ran up, dipped back and kissed the EMAs, then a green candle closed back above EMA20: that's the signal candle (yellow dot, ~24,244). We don't buy it — the next candle pushes past its close, giving the proof, so we BUY the CE at 24,266. The trade rides up to ~24,344; when a later candle dips back and its range touches the climbing EMA21 trail, the trend has broken and we EXIT at 24,304 for +38 spot points.

What we DON'T take. This green candle (yellow dot) closes up and looks tempting, but check 1 fails: the blue EMA20 is still below the purple EMA50 — there is no confirmed up-trend, just chop. One good-looking candle is never enough; all four checks must line up, so this is no trade. Skipping setups like this — where the trend lines aren't stacked our way — is most of what keeps the strategy out of sideways days.

The RSI numbers above (52–80 for CE, 20–48 for PE) are the built-in code defaults. The saved Settings page uses a slightly tighter band (ceiling 70 for CE, floor 30 for PE) — so a real install is a touch more cautious than these raw defaults. The "not overheated" ceiling is what keeps us from jumping onto a move that has already run too far.

05 — Step 2: Wait for one candle of proof

Here's the part that keeps us off fakeouts: we don't buy the moment the checklist passes. The candle that passes all four checks is only the signal. We then wait for the very next candle to keep pushing the same way — specifically, to trade past the signal candle's closing price. The instant it does, we buy (we don't even wait for that candle to finish).

Why bother waiting?

Lots of candles look great right as they close and then immediately reverse — a classic one-candle head-fake. Asking the next candle to push further in our direction is cheap, quick proof that the move is real and not just a single flicker. It costs us a few points of "best entry," and in return it filters out a whole category of instant losers.

This waiting behaviour is on by default (EMA_RSI_ST_CONFIRM_CANDLE_ENABLED). If you turn it off, the strategy reverts to the older style of buying the instant the four checks pass, without waiting for proof.

06 — Step 3: The moving safety line

Every trade carries a safety line (a stop-loss): if the price hits it, we're out. It starts in one place and then climbs along behind the trade, only ever tightening.

Where it starts

The moment we buy, the safety line is set to the previous candle's low (for a CE) or its high (for a PE). That's a natural "if price falls back through here, I was wrong" level — used as-is, with no fixed cap.

How it climbs — the EMA21 trail

After that, at the close of every candle, the safety line is moved up to the EMA21 line (a 21-candle trend average). It only ever moves in the winning direction — up for a CE, down for a PE — and never backs off. Because the safety line hugs EMA21, a candle that dips back and touches EMA21 is treated as the trend breaking, and we exit (more on that in Section 7).

Two optional tighteners

Candle trail: you can also track the lowest low of the last 3 candles (for a CE) and use whichever line is tighter — the EMA21 or that recent low. It banks more of a winner. (Off in the raw code, but the saved Settings page turns it on.)

Negative-candle stop: if a trade is still showing a loss (the option is worth less than we paid) at the close of 2 candles, we simply cut it. The idea is deliberately lopsided: losers get shown the door quickly, while winners are left alone to keep riding the trail.

An optional break-even floor (off by default)

There's also a switch-on-if-you-want break-even stop. When it's on and a trade gets EMA_RSI_ST_BREAKEVEN_PTS points in profit (measured on the NIFTY spot, checked at each candle close), the safety line is lifted straight up to the exact price we bought at. From then on the worst case is roughly a scratch — a winner can no longer flip back into a loss. Like everything else here it only ever tightens: if the EMA21 trail has already climbed past our entry, this floor changes nothing.

A quick example

We buy a CE with NIFTY spot at 24,358, and break-even is set to +25 points. NIFTY climbs to 24,383 at a candle close — that's +25, so the safety line jumps up to 24,358, our entry. Now if the move dies and NIFTY slides back, we're taken out at roughly break-even instead of at a loss. A PE works the same way, just mirrored downward.

This is off by default (EMA_RSI_ST_BREAKEVEN_ENABLED=false). It's the deliberate opt-in replacement for the old always-on "move-to-break-even" that used to be built in and was removed. When switched on it runs identically in practice, live and backtest. With it off, the safety line's job is done entirely by the starting level, the EMA21 trail and the optional tighteners above.

07 — Step 4: When we sell

There's no fixed profit target — we never say "take +40% and walk away." Instead several exit rules watch the trade at once, and whichever one triggers first closes it. Some are checked on every price tick; the rest are checked when a candle finishes.

What sells us outWhenIn plain words
Points stopevery tickA hard "catastrophe" cap: if NIFTY moves a set number of points against us, we bail immediately. Off by default (turned on in the saved Settings, at 25 points).
Option meltdown stopevery tickIf the option's price falls 15% below what we paid, we're out — even if NIFTY hasn't hit our other stops. This catches the "the option just bled away" losses.
Safety line hitevery tickThe main stop: price reaches the trailing safety line (the EMA21 trail, plus the optional tighteners). Whichever line is tighter wins.
Negative-candle stopcandle closeStill losing after 2 finished candles → cut it. Winners are never touched by this rule — they keep riding the trail.
EMA21 touch-backcandle closeIf a candle's range dips back and touches the EMA21 line, the trend has broken — we exit. (Ignored on the very first candle, so we don't get shaken out instantly.)
Opposite signalcandle closeWe're holding a CE and a fresh PE setup appears (or vice-versa) — the market has flipped, so we exit.
Exit before closecandle closeSquare off any open trade at a set afternoon time (EMA_RSI_ST_EOD_EXIT_TIME), ahead of the final bell.
End of day15:30Anything still open is sold and the engine stops. We never hold overnight.

A worked example — a CE trend day

10:35  A 5-min candle closes at 24,352.
       - EMA20 (24,330) is above EMA50 (24,300)        -> trend up   [check 1]
       - RSI is 63 (above 52, below 80)                -> strong     [check 2]
       - SuperTrend line sits below price              -> up         [check 3]
       - The candle closed at 24,352, above EMA20      -> on-side    [check 4]
       All four pass. This is the SIGNAL candle. We do NOT buy yet.

10:40  The next candle trades up through 24,352 (the signal's close).
       -> That's our proof.  BUY a CE.  Spot ~24,358, premium paid = Rs 200.
       First safety line = the previous candle's low, 24,340.
       Meltdown line = 15% below Rs 200 = Rs 170 (a Rs 30-per-unit drop).

11:15  Trade is nicely up; each finished candle drags EMA21 higher,
       so the safety line keeps climbing behind price. We just hold.

12:30  Spot is 24,430, the option is worth Rs 300. Still trending. Hold.

13:05  A candle finally dips back and its range TOUCHES the EMA21 line.
       -> Trend broken.  SELL the CE at premium Rs 280.

       Result: Rs 280 - Rs 200 = Rs 80 per unit.
       One lot = 75 units, so profit = 80 x 75 = Rs 6,000.
       The meltdown stop (Rs 170) and points stop never had to fire.
Why no fixed target? If we always sold at "+40%," we'd cut our biggest trend days short — and a few big days are what pay for all the small losing ones over a month. The trailing safety line keeps us in the move as long as it lasts, while the meltdown stop, points stop and negative-candle stop make sure a bad trade dies small.

08 — The cool-downs: no chasing the same loss

Choppy, sideways days are where a trend-follower bleeds — it keeps getting tricked into buying, then stopped out, over and over. Three "cool-down" rules exist to stop that from snowballing.

Cool-downWhat it does
Same-side pauseAfter a stop-out on one side (say a CE), we refuse to buy that same side again for a few candles. Stops us re-buying the exact setup that just lost, tick after tick. (Default 3 candles; the saved Settings use 2.)
Opposite-side (flip) pauseAfter any stop-type exit, we also refuse to immediately flip to the other side for a few candles. This is the big one — it kills the "CE stopped out, buy PE, PE stopped out, buy CE…" whipsaw that quietly drains an account on a choppy day. It's on by default. (A normal opposite-signal exit or an end-of-day exit does not trigger this pause.)
Choppy-day brakeAn optional switch: after a set number of losing trades in a row in one session, stop taking new trades for the rest of the day. Any single winner resets the count. Off by default.

09 — Safety limits & the "too jumpy" filter

On top of the per-trade stops, a few account-level limits act like circuit breakers.

LimitWhat it does
Daily loss cap (MAX_DAILY_LOSS)Once the day's losses reach a set rupee amount, stop taking new trades. (Code default ₹5,000; the saved Settings use a tighter ₹3,000.) In live trading this cap now survives a restart — if the bot crashes or redeploys mid-day, it re-reads today's already-booked live loss and stays stopped, instead of resetting the budget to zero.
Portfolio-wide loss cap (PORTFOLIO_MAX_DAILY_LOSS)A cap across all six strategies at once, not just this one. It adds up today's booked profit-and-loss from every strategy; once the combined loss reaches the cap, new trades are blocked in every strategy for the rest of the day. It can only ever block a new entry — it never places or changes an order — and if it can't read the numbers it lets trading continue. Off by default (0 = off).
Max trades per day (MAX_DAILY_TRADES)A hard cap on how many trades can be opened in one session — an anti-over-trading brake. (Code default 20; saved Settings use 5.)
Trading window (TRADE_ENTRY_START / TRADE_ENTRY_END)New trades only allowed inside set hours (roughly late morning to early afternoon). No entries outside the window.
VIX filter (VIX_FILTER_ENABLED / VIX_MAX_ENTRY)The "too jumpy" filter: block new trades when the market's fear gauge (VIX) is above the limit — those days swing hard and options get expensive.
OI check (EMA_RSI_ST_OI_ENABLED) liveOptional: before a real trade, require the big-money option positioning (open interest) to support our direction. Live only, off by default.
Wide-spread skip liveSkip a real trade if the option's buy/sell gap is too wide to enter cleanly. Live only.
Keeping two numbers in sync: the option-meltdown stop is 15% of the premium. On a typical ₹200 option that's about ₹30 per unit, or roughly ₹2,250 on one lot (75 units). Two of those in a day already get close to a small daily-loss cap — so if you tighten one, sanity-check the other.

11 — Expiry & going live (real money)

  • No trading on expiry day itself. If the option's expiry date is today, the strategy refuses to start — holding an option through its own expiry is too risky. (A replay of a past day is allowed to bypass this.)
  • TRADE_EXPIRY_DAY_ONLY — the opposite switch: when on, only trade on NIFTY expiry day.
  • EMA_RSI_ST_OPTION_EXPIRY_OVERRIDE / _TYPE let this strategy trade a different weekly expiry from the other strategies. Left blank, it just follows the shared expiry.
  • Live trading has two locks. Real Zerodha orders only go out when EMA_RSI_ST_LIVE_ENABLED=true and the global dry-run switch (LIVE_HARNESS_DRY_RUN) is off. While dry-run is on (the default), the system shows the order it would have placed but sends nothing. Only turn dry-run off after checking that live and paper make the same decisions on a replayed day.
  • Clean shutdown squares off first. When the process is asked to stop (a deploy or restart signal), any open live position is now squared off before it exits — a redeploy can't leave a real trade stranded. And because several strategies share one price feed, stopping this one no longer tears that feed away from another strategy that still has a live position running.

12 — What changed from the old strategy

This strategy used to be called "SWING" and was more complicated. Over time it was simplified down to the clean four-check version in this guide. The biggest change: an indicator called Parabolic SAR was fully removed, and SuperTrend is now the only direction source. (The code file is still named strategy1_sar_ema_rsi.js for historical reasons — but there is no SAR left in it.)

Taken out:

Parabolic SAR EMA21-touch entry EMA30 trend gate ADX filter candle-body filter SAR-distance rules "strong / marginal" grading 3-tier trailing always-on move-to-break-even

In place now:

EMA20 vs EMA50 trend check optional EMA9>20>50 stack SuperTrend direction close-beyond-the-EMA check wait-for-proof candle EMA21 trailing safety line optional recent-lows trail negative-candle stop EMA21 touch-back exit optional break-even floor (off)

Bottom line: the strategy is now four clear checks → one candle of proof → ride the trend behind a climbing safety line. Simple to follow, and every mode (practice, live, backtest, replay) runs the exact same logic.
