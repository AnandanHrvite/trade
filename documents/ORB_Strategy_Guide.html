⚙ 1. The idea in one minute

Every morning, in its first 15 minutes (9:15 to 9:30), NIFTY draws a small high-and-low box. Think of it as the market "making up its mind." This strategy watches that box and asks one question: does the price break out of the box and keep running?

  • If price breaks above the box and keeps going up → we buy a CE (call option) to profit from the rise.
  • If price breaks below the box and keeps falling → we buy a PE (put option) to profit from the drop.

We buy only one option at a time (no pairs, no hedges), and normally only one trade for the whole day. The whole point is to catch a trending day — a day that picks a direction in the morning and runs with it — and to skip the days that just wobble sideways and trap you.

The version running today: "V3 — the trend-day engine" (built 10 July 2026). Its goal is simple: catch the strong trend days and throw away the fake breakouts — not to trade more often. It buys options that are slightly in-the-money (explained below), and it measures everything against how much NIFTY is normally moving that day, so the same settings keep working whether the market is calm or wild. Two older versions (V2 and V1) still exist as a fallback, but everything in this guide describes V3.

Why only one trade a day?

The morning box only forms once. A "breakout" that shows up at 2 PM is usually a completely different situation (news, a late surge) and tends to be a coin-flip. So V3 commits to a single breakout: the first clean one it sees. If that one doesn't work out, it does not go looking for a second try. Trying to squeeze in more trades just adds afternoon chop to the results.

📚 2. Words you'll see (plain meanings)

A few terms come up again and again. Here's what each one means, in everyday language — keep this handy and the rest of the guide reads easily.

TermWhat it really means
Opening range (OR)The high and low of NIFTY's first 15 minutes. The "box" the market draws while it makes up its mind. "OR width" = how tall the box is, in points.
CE / PECE (call) makes money when NIFTY goes up. PE (put) makes money when NIFTY goes down. We only ever buy one of them at a time.
CandleA 5-minute price bar showing where NIFTY opened, closed, and its high/low in that 5 minutes. A "green" candle closed higher than it opened; "red" closed lower.
ATR"Average True Range" — simply how much NIFTY normally moves in a bar. If ATR is 30, a typical 5-minute candle spans about 30 points. We use it as a ruler: a "big" move on a calm day is small on a wild day, and ATR tells us which is which.
VWAPThe day's fair-value line — the average price weighted by how much traded there. If price is above VWAP, buyers are in control; below it, sellers are. We only buy CE above the line, PE below it.
In-the-money (ITM) optionAn option that already has real value baked in, so it moves almost point-for-point with NIFTY and loses value slowly. We buy slightly ITM because it tracks a trend better than a cheap far-away option.
PremiumThe price you pay for the option (in ₹ per unit). "Premium collapsed 35%" means the option lost about a third of its value.
Bid-ask spreadThe gap between the buy price and the sell price of an option. A wide gap means you lose money just entering and exiting — so we skip options with a wide spread.
VIXThe market's "fear gauge." High VIX = jumpy, expensive options and bigger swings. We can tell the strategy to sit out when VIX is too high.
EMA20A smoothed average of the last several closes (the "20" is how many). We use it as a moving trend line: as long as price stays on the right side of it, the trend is still alive.

🕔 3. Step 1 — Draw the opening box

From 9:15 to 9:30, the strategy watches every price tick and records the highest high and lowest low. At 9:30 sharp, the box is locked for the rest of the day — the top of the box (ORH) and bottom (ORL) never change again, no matter what happens later.

SettingDefaultWhat it does
ORB_RANGE_START09:15When the box starts (market open).
ORB_RANGE_END09:30When the box locks. After this, the top and bottom are fixed.
ORB_ENTRY_END11:30Latest we'll take a new trade. A breakout after this is "too late" — the day is half over. (The older V1/V2 versions used 12:00.)
ORB_FORCED_EXIT15:15If we're still holding anything at this time, sell it — win or lose. We never carry overnight.
ORB_ATR_PERIOD14How many bars go into the "how much NIFTY normally moves" ruler (ATR).
ORB_EXPIRY_DAY_ONLYfalseIf turned on, only trade on weekly-expiry day (Tuesday).

First question: is the box a good one?

Not every morning box is worth trading. Before looking for a breakout, the strategy checks the box itself and can decide to skip the whole day:

  • Is the box a sensible size? Compared to how much NIFTY is normally moving (ATR), the box shouldn't be too tiny (that's just sideways noise) or too huge (the big move already happened before 9:30, so we'd be chasing). It has to sit between 0.7× and 2.5× the normal move.
  • Did the market gap open crazily? If NIFTY opened miles away from yesterday's close (more than 3× the box height), that's usually news or panic that has already spent its energy — skip the day.
Why measure against "the normal move" instead of fixed points? A 40-point box means something very different on a sleepy day than on a stormy one. By comparing the box to how much NIFTY is actually moving today, the same settings keep making sense in any market — you never have to re-tune numbers when volatility changes.

▶ 4. Step 2 — The checklist before a trade

For a trade to happen, every box below has to tick "yes," in order. If any one fails, we skip — and the reason is written into the log so you can see exactly why nothing traded today.

Price must also break past yesterday's high (for CE) or low (for PE). That's where trapped traders are forced to buy/sell — extra fuel for the move. This is the strictest check; turn it off if it blocks too many days.
CE: close above yesterday's high  |  PE: close below yesterday's low

big body · closes near the high/low · CE above VWAP, PE below VWAP
6
Wait for one more candle to agree
We do NOT buy the breakout candle itself. We wait for the next candle to keep pushing in the same direction. This one extra step is what keeps us on the real trend days and off the fakeouts.
next candle makes a higher high & higher close (CE) / lower low & lower close (PE)
7
A backup way in (optional)
If that next candle hesitates, we get a short second window to enter on a pullback-and-hold or a fresh push. But this is a bonus path — it never blocks a trade (see Section 5).
optional retest within the next few candles
8
Is the option worth buying?
Pick a slightly in-the-money strike, make sure its price is in a sensible range, and that the buy/sell gap (spread) is tight. (Live/paper only — history has no option prices.)
premium between ₹120 and ₹400 · spread ≤ 2 pts
9
Haven't we already traded today?
One trade per day. And only inside the window (after 9:30, before 11:30).
trades today = 0 · time is 09:30–11:30
What changed from the old version: the previous engine (V2) also demanded a stack of extra indicators (EMA20/50, ADX, RSI). Testing showed those filters mostly cancelled each other out and, worse, they threw away the big runaway winners. So V3 dropped them and relies on the structural checks above — the box, the fresh-ground break, the strong candle, and the confirmation.

🔄 5. Step 3 — Wait for proof, then buy

The strategy never jumps on the breakout candle itself. It waits for proof. There are two ways it can decide to actually buy:

The main way — confirmation

After a strong breakout candle, we look at the very next 5-minute candle. If it keeps pushing the same way (a higher high and higher close for a CE, still above the fair-value line), that's our proof — we buy when that candle closes. This catches the days that break out and just keep going.

The backup way — the optional "retest"

Sometimes the next candle stalls instead of pushing. Rather than give up, the strategy keeps watching for a few more candles and can still get in if price:

  • Pushes back up in the breakout direction (the trend resumes), or
  • Dips back to the box edge, touches it, and bounces the right way (a "retest" that holds), or
  • Is still clearly trending when the short backup window ends.
Important: this backup is optional and never blocks a trade. If price simply rockets away and never comes back to retest, we still buy — we don't wait for a pullback that may never come. We tried the opposite (forcing a retest before every trade) and it actually made results worse: demanding a pullback kept us in the weak, stall-and-fade breakouts and made us miss the strong ones that never look back — which are exactly the trades that make the whole strategy profitable. So the retest is only ever a helpful fallback, never a rule.

Why we wait for proof. Price poked above the box top on one green candle — but it never got clearly above the fair-value (VWAP) line, and the very next candle slammed straight back down inside the box. The confirmation never came, so we never bought. The orange dot marks the failed poke: a classic fakeout that the confirmation-plus-VWAP rule quietly filters out.

Once we've decided to trade, we still have to choose which option to buy and check it's worth trading.

In the backtest, there are no real option prices. History doesn't record option quotes, so the backtest estimates the option: it starts from a typical slightly-ITM price (₹240) and models how it would move with NIFTY. To stay honest it also charges a spread/slippage haircut on both entry and exit (ORB_BT_SLIPPAGE_PTS, default 1.5 pts each way) and fills a blown stop at the gap-open when a candle jumps clean through it, not at the stop price — so a violent bar is costed realistically. Backtest profit is still a good approximation, not the exact fills you'd get live — always confirm against paper trading.

🚫 7. Step 5 — When we sell

The exits are built to let a winner run while cutting the losers quickly. There is no fixed profit target — we don't cap the trade at "+40%" and walk away. Instead, a handful of rules watch the trade, and whichever one triggers first closes it:

What sells us outDefaultIn plain words
Starting stop-loss—Right after we buy, our safety line is the low of our entry candle (for CE) or its high (for PE). If price falls back through that, we're out.
Move to break-even~20 pts profitOnce the trade is comfortably in profit (about 20 points, more on a wide-box day), we slide the stop up to our entry price. From here the trade can't turn into a loss.
Trend trail (EMA20)EMA 20The main exit. We ride the trend and only sell when a candle closes back across the moving trend line. One quick dip that closes back on-side does not shake us out — the trend has to genuinely break.
Strong candle against usonIf a big candle slams back the other way and closes inside the box, that's a clear rejection — we exit.
Rupee loss cap₹1,500A hard floor: if the trade is losing ₹1,500, we're out, no matter what the candles say.
Option melted down−35%If the option price collapses 35% from what we paid (even if NIFTY hasn't hit our spot stop), we bail. This catches the "the option bled out on time-decay" losses.
End of day15:15Anything still open at 3:15 PM is sold. We never hold overnight.
There's a "target" line on the chart, but it's just for looking. You'll see a target level drawn on the ORB page (1.5× the box height beyond the edge). Nothing actually sells when price reaches it — the trend trail decides how far a winner runs.

A worked example — a CE trend day

9:30   Box locks: top 24,325 / bottom 24,265  (60 points tall).
       Normal move today is ~40, so 60 is a healthy box (1.5×)      → good day
       Market opened near yesterday's close, no crazy gap           → good day
       Yesterday's high was 24,300 (below the box top)              → fresh ground is live

9:40   A candle closes at 24,352 — clearly above the box top + cushion,
       AND above yesterday's high 24,300. Big green body, closes near its high,
       above the fair-value line.  →  This is the day's breakout.
       (We do NOT buy yet.)

9:45   Next candle pushes higher and closes at 24,371.  →  Proof.  BUY a CE.
       Option is 1-step in-the-money, priced ₹190, tight spread.
       Safety line = this candle's low, 24,340.

10:05  Trade is +32 points in profit  →  stop moves up to our entry (can't lose now).
10:40  Trend keeps running; every candle stays above the EMA20 line. We just hold.
13:10  Finally a candle CLOSES below the EMA20 line  →  SELL. Trend's over.
       Big win banked. The rupee-cap and meltdown stops never had to fire.

The iconic ORB picture. The blue dashed box is the 9:15–9:30 opening range. Price coiled inside it, then a big green candle closed clearly above the box top and above the fair-value (VWAP) line (orange dot = the breakout candle). We still didn't buy — only when the next candle pushed higher did we get our proof → BUY CE at 24,372. We then rode the orange EMA20 trend line up and sold the moment a candle closed back below EMA20 → EXIT 24,378. No fixed target — the trend trail decided how far the winner ran.

Why no fixed target? If we always sold at "+40%," we'd throw away the big trend days — and a few of those big days are what make the whole strategy profitable over a month. The trend trail keeps us in the move as long as it lasts, while the break-even, rupee-cap and meltdown stops make sure a bad trade dies small. The real skill is in the entry checklist — the exit just decides how much of a good entry we get to keep.

🔅 8. The "market too jumpy" filter (VIX)

VIX is the market's fear gauge. When it's high, options get expensive and swings get violent — a riskier time to trade. ORB has its own optional VIX filter to sit out those days.

SettingDefaultIn plain words
ORB_VIX_ENABLEDfalseThe on/off switch. Off by default, so VIX is ignored unless you turn it on.
ORB_VIX_MAX_ENTRY22If VIX is above 22, don't trade at all today — too jumpy.
ORB_VIX_STRONG_ONLY18Between 18 and 22, only take the very best setups.
Why ORB has its own VIX limits: because we hold the trade for hours with no fixed target, our option's value is especially sensitive to how jumpy the market is. High-VIX days can swing hard against us, so ORB uses its own ceiling rather than the shared one.

🛡 9. Safety limits & the losing-streak brake

Several limits protect the account. Think of them as circuit-breakers of increasing size — per trade, per day, and per week.

LimitDefaultIn plain words
One trade a day1After today's trade, we're done — a failed trade is not a reason to try again.
Most we'll lose on one trade₹1,500Hard stop on a single trade's loss.
Option-meltdown stop−35%Exit if the option loses 35% of its value (time-decay / volatility crush).
Daily loss brake₹3,000Stop taking new trades once the day's loss hits ₹3,000. (With one trade a day, rarely matters.)
Weekly loss brake₹9,000If the week's losses reach ₹9,000, sit out the rest of the week (Monday–Friday).
Losing-streak brake4 daysAfter 4 losing days in a row, take a one-day break to reset.
Latest new trade11:30No fresh entries after 11:30 — the day's too far gone.
Sell everything by15:15Close any open trade at 3:15 PM.
Skip wide spreads2 ptsDon't trade an option whose buy/sell gap is too wide.
The weekly & streak brakes are new in V3. The strategy remembers its recent losses (saved to disk, so it survives a restart) and will stop itself from trading during a bad run — even across days. Practice and live mode keep separate memories, so a rough patch in practice never freezes your live trading, and vice-versa.

Crash-recovery (survives a restart)

ORB now writes its open trade to disk the moment it's opened (.active_orb_position.json) and clears it on close. If the server crashes or is redeployed with a trade still open, on the next boot the system re-reads that snapshot, checks it against your Fyers positions, and Telegrams you if it finds an orphan — and only clears the snapshot once the broker book is proven readable and flat. ORB previously had no such memory (a crash left an untracked position); it now matches the other five engines.

Portfolio-wide daily loss cap default OFF Separate from ORB's own ORB_MAX_DAILY_LOSS, there's now a single cap across all six strategies (PORTFOLIO_MAX_DAILY_LOSS, default 0 = off). It adds up today's realised P&L from every practice mode; once the combined loss hits the cap, it blocks new entries in every strategy (ORB included) for the rest of the day. It only ever blocks — it never places or changes an order — and if it can't read the totals it fails open (lets trading continue).

📦 10. The older versions (V2 / V1)

V3 (described in this guide) is what runs by default. The two earlier designs are still in the code as a safety net — if you ever wanted to compare, you can switch back with ORB_ENTRY_V3_ENABLED=false. In short:

VersionWhat it did differently
V2 — "confirmed breakout"Same breakout idea, but it piled on extra indicator checks (EMA20/50, ADX, RSI). Those turned out to cancel each other and throw away the big winners, so V3 dropped them.
V1 — "the textbook version"The original: it bought immediately on the breakout candle (no waiting for proof), used fixed point sizes for the box, and graded trades as "strong" or "marginal." Simple, but it took too many fakeouts.
A few checks apply no matter which version you run: the VIX filter, the crowd-positioning (OI) check, the option price/spread checks, and the expiry-day-only option. The old fixed-point settings simply do nothing while V3 is on.

⚙ 11. Paper, Live & Backtest

Paper — /orb-paper (practice)

Real live prices, pretend money. No real orders are ever placed. This is the "official" version of the strategy — whenever live or backtest behaves differently, paper is treated as correct. Every trade is saved to your trading-data folder, and every price tick can be recorded so you can replay the exact day later.

Live — /orb-live (real money)

Real orders through Fyers — but locked behind two safety switches so it can never fire by accident: ORB_LIVE_ENABLED must be on and the global dry-run switch must be off. While dry-run is on (the default), the system shows the order it would place but doesn't actually send it. Only turn dry-run off after you've checked that live and paper make the same decisions on a replayed day.

Optional exchange-side disaster stop default OFF For live (harness) trades you can also rest a backstop stop-loss at the exchange (HARNESS_EXCHANGE_SL_ENABLED, with HARNESS_SL_PCT the trigger as a % of your entry premium). It's a safety net in case the bot crashes mid-trade — the normal in-app stops stay in charge. The resting order is always cancelled before any normal sell, so it can't fire on a position you're already exiting. It places real orders, so validate it on a dry-run session first.

Backtest — /orb-backtest (test on the past)

Runs the strategy over past dates to see how it would have done. Since history has no option prices, it estimates the option's price and movement (see Section 6). You get a downloadable summary at the end.

Replay — /replay (re-run a recorded day)

Takes a day you recorded in paper mode and runs it again, tick by tick. You can replay it with the original settings (to reproduce exactly what happened) or with your current settings (to test a tweak against the same real prices, after market hours). Great for safely trying out changes.

⚠ 13. What's still missing

  • Backtest option prices are estimated. Since history has no option quotes, backtest profit is an approximation, not exact fills. Always sanity-check against paper/replay.
  • Results are lumpy by design. ORB's profit comes from a handful of big trend days. Expect quiet, flat stretches between them — judge it over a few weeks, not a few days.
  • The "fresh ground" check is strict. Requiring the break to clear yesterday's high/low will skip some otherwise-clean days. It's on because those trapped-trader setups are the best ones — but you can turn it off if it's too restrictive for you.
Recently fixed (used to be on this list): there's now a per-trade rupee stop and an option-meltdown stop; a buy/sell-spread check on the option; a weekly / losing-streak brake that pauses trading in a bad run; the crowd-positioning (OI) check now behaves the same in practice and live; and crash-recovery now works for ORB too — an open trade survives a server restart (see Section 9).
